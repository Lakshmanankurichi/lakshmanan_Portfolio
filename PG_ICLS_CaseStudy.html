01 — Context

This platform doesn't just track research. It runs P&G's decisions.

Budget allocations. Production runs. Distribution plans. New product launches. Every one of these decisions traces back to data collected through ICLS — the Integrated Consumer Learning Solutions platform.

7 Fragmented Tools

Research teams operated across seven disconnected systems with incompatible data models, access patterns, and business logic. Studies were duplicated. Insights got lost.

5 Stakeholder Groups

Study owners, administrators, design agencies, suppliers, and research analysts — each with different workflows, priorities, and assumptions about how the platform should work.

15-Year Legacy Backend

Undocumented dependencies made every change a calculated risk. Replacing the backend was completely off the table. Every improvement had to be pure UX.

Broken Flows, Real Costs

Multiple navigation patterns, missing notifications, and 12-step forms that should take 5. When researchers abandon forms, P&G's decision data gets incomplete. The cost is downstream.

"We have the tools, but nobody knows how to use them. We have the data, but nobody can find it. We have the research, but it doesn't inform decisions."
— Senior Product Manager, P&G · Week 1 Discovery

04 — Key Decisions

Why I made these choices

Senior UX work is about the decisions nobody sees. Here are the four that shaped every outcome.

D1
The situation
Five teams, five different realities
Stakeholders had conflicting assumptions about how consumer research should work. Starting design would have meant redesigning everything twice as assumptions surfaced mid-build.
The decision
Spend 2 months on alignment before any UI
Facilitation is UX. The workshops I ran in month 1 and 2 weren't overhead — they were the most valuable design work of the entire project. They prevented 4 months of rework.
D2
The situation
Complex forms, high abandonment
The CRP form was the most-used and most-abandoned flow. The instinct was to add help text, tooltips, and onboarding overlays to make it clearer.
The decision
Remove steps, don't explain them
A user who doesn't need instructions is better than a user who reads great instructions. Reduced the form from 12 steps to 5. Then added AI assistance for what remained.
D3
The situation
15-year-old backend, can't be changed
Every change was a calculated risk. The constraint felt like a hard ceiling on what was achievable. The easy answer was to ask for backend changes.
The decision
Treat the constraint as a design brief
No backend crutch meant every decision had to justify itself purely on user value. This produced simpler, more elegant solutions — and proved that legacy systems aren't the enemy. Poor UX on top of them is.
D4
The situation
Pressure to add AI everywhere
AI was available. The stakeholder ask was to "make it AI-powered." The easy path was to add AI across the entire platform as a headline feature.
The decision
AI at the exact point of highest friction
Placed AI only in form-filling — the moment users were most likely to abandon or rush, producing bad data. Specificity made it immediately valuable. Phase 2 will expand from this proven foundation.

06 — Reflection

What this project taught me

Seven months, five stakeholder groups, a 15-year legacy backend, and a platform that shapes billion-dollar decisions. Here's what stayed with me.

01
Ambiguity is the work
Early in my career, ambiguity was something to eliminate quickly so I could "get to the real work" of designing. Now I understand: the clarity you create in ambiguous situations is the highest-value thing a senior designer produces. The platform's first two months produced no screens — and those two months were the most impactful of the seven.
02
Facilitation is a design skill
The most valuable thing I designed wasn't a screen — it was the workshop that gave five stakeholder groups a shared language. Facilitation, synthesis, and building shared understanding are core UX competencies, not soft skills to delegate. This project proved it in measurable terms: 2 months of alignment prevented 4 months of rework.
03
Legacy systems aren't the enemy
The 15-year backend was fine. Seven years of accumulated complexity in the UI on top of it was what was breaking users. The constraint forced cleaner thinking. Every design decision had to justify itself purely on user value — no technical shortcuts. Constraint drives creativity when you lean into it rather than around it.
04
Scale changes the stakes
This platform doesn't serve consumers — it shapes decisions about what consumers experience. When researchers submit better data, P&G makes better product decisions. Every friction point I removed had a business cost far downstream. Designing at enterprise scale means understanding that the user's problem and the business problem are the same problem.
